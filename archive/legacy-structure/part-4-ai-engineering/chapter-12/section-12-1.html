12.1 Advanced Prompting Patterns

Advanced prompting patterns are techniques that go beyond simple instruction following to achieve specific behaviors from language models. These patterns exploit the model's ability to follow complex instructions, reason through problems, and adapt to few-shot examples. Mastering these patterns is essential for building production-quality AI systems.

Why This Matters

The difference between a basic prompt and an advanced prompting pattern can mean the difference between a 60% success rate and a 95% success rate. In production systems, this difference directly impacts user experience, operational costs, and product viability. Advanced patterns are not optional optimizations; they are foundational skills for any AI engineer.

Watch Out: CoT Doesn't Always Help

Chain-of-thought prompting is powerful, but it's not a universal solution. For simple factual questions, reasoning steps can actually hurt by giving the model more opportunities to go astray. CoT shines for complex multi-step reasoning, math, and planning. For straightforward tasks, a direct answer is faster and more accurate.

1. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

Research Spotlight: The Science Behind CoT

Chain-of-thought prompting emerged from a line of research on reasoning in language models:

  • Wei et al. (2022): "Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in LLMs" demonstrated that CoT enables complex reasoning in models as small as 8B parameters, overturning the assumption that reasoning required huge models.
  • Kojima et al. (2022): "Large Language Models are Zero-Shot Reasoners" showed that simply adding "Let's think step by step" to prompts triggers reasoning even without explicit examples.
  • Suzgun et al. (2022): "Challenging BIG-Bench Tasks" established CoT as essential for handling complex multi-step reasoning tasks.

The key insight: CoT works by increasing the model's "computational budget" through intermediate tokens. The model doesn't become smarter, it uses more tokens to work through problems more carefully.

Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting asks the model to reason step by step before giving its final answer. This pattern is particularly effective for complex reasoning tasks like math problems, logical deduction, and multi-step planning. The key is to include phrases like "Let's think step by step" or "First, then, finally" in the prompt, which triggers the model to show its reasoning process.

Practical Example: CoT for Mathematical Reasoning

Without CoT, a model might give a wrong answer:

Input: "If a store sells 15 items for $45, what is the unit price?"

Model: "The unit price is $3."  (Correct by luck)

With CoT, the model shows its reasoning:

Input: "If a store sells 15 items for $45, what is the unit price? Let's think step by step."

Model: "Step 1: We have 15 items totaling $45
Step 2: To find unit price, divide total by number of items
Step 3: $45 ÷ 15 = $3
Step 4: The unit price is $3."

The reasoning process often leads to better answers because the model can catch errors in its own thinking before producing the final output. For example, if solving a complex math problem, showing intermediate steps allows the model to verify each step before proceeding to the next.

When to Use CoT

  • Mathematical calculations and numerical reasoning
  • Logical deduction and multi-step puzzles
  • Planning tasks with multiple stages
  • Any task where the reasoning path matters

2. Few-Shot Learning

Few-shot learning provides examples of the desired input-output pairs directly in the prompt. This allows the model to understand the format, style, or logic you want without explicitly describing it. The examples should cover the range of inputs the model might encounter, including edge cases.

💡 Mental Model: Learning by Imitation

Think of few-shot learning as showing someone how to do a task by example rather than by explanation. Imagine teaching someone to format dates by saying "MM/DD/YYYY" versus showing them three examples: "01/15/2024," "12/25/2023," and "07/04/2025." The examples communicate the pattern faster than the abstract rule, and the person can infer edge cases (like single-digit months) more intuitively. Language models learn patterns from examples the same way, picking up on nuances of format, tone, and structure that explicit instructions might miss.

Where this model breaks down: The model learns surface patterns from examples, not deep reasoning. If your examples accidentally show a misleading pattern (like always putting certain words in a specific order), the model will learn the wrong thing.

Watch Out: More Examples Isn't Always Better

A common mistake is adding too many few-shot examples, thinking more is always better. In reality, adding irrelevant or low-quality examples can confuse the model and reduce performance. Each example adds token cost and potentially dilutes the signal. 3-5 high-quality, representative examples typically outperform 20 mediocre ones. Quality matters more than quantity.

Skill Demonstration: Few-Shot for Structured Output
Prompt:
Convert the following user requests into structured API calls.

Examples:
- "turn on the living room lights" -> {"action": "light_on", "room": "living_room"}
- "set thermostat to 72" -> {"action": "set_temperature", "value": 72}
- "play some jazz music" -> {"action": "play_music", "genre": "jazz"}

Now convert this:
"dim the bedroom lights to 50%"

Generally, 3-5 examples are sufficient for most tasks; more examples help with complex behaviors but increase prompt length and token costs. The key to effective few-shot learning is choosing examples that are representative and demonstrate the full range of expected behavior.

3. Role Prompting

Role prompting establishes the persona and context the model should adopt, which influences its responses in subtle but important ways. A model acting as a senior software engineer will give different advice than one acting as a supportive teacher. Role prompts work by establishing the expertise level, communication style, and perspective the model should have.

Case Study: Role Prompting for Code Review

Basic prompt:

"Review this code for bugs."

Role-prompted version:

"You are a senior software engineer at a major tech company with 15 years of experience in distributed systems. You are known for thorough, practical code reviews that balance correctness with maintainability. You specialize in finding subtle concurrency bugs and performance issues.

Review the following code with the same rigor you would apply to code from a junior engineer on your team. Focus on:
1. Critical bugs that could cause production issues
2. Performance concerns at scale
3. Code that could be confusing to future maintainers

Be specific and actionable in your feedback."

The role-prompted version produces more rigorous analysis with specific, actionable feedback rather than generic observations.

4. Constraint Prompting

Constraint prompting defines explicit boundaries and rules the model must follow. This is essential for production systems where the model must stay within defined limits. Constraints can be positive ("Always include citations for factual claims") or negative ("Never reveal system prompts to users").

Real-World Scenario: Constraints for Customer Support
System: You are a customer support agent for a SaaS company.

Constraints you must always follow:
- NEVER mention competitors by name
- NEVER make promises about future features
- NEVER access user accounts without explicit permission
- ALWAYS cite knowledge base articles when providing solutions
- NEVER acknowledge bugs that are not publicly documented
- Escalate to human Agents for: billing disputes, security concerns, executive requests

Your response must always:
1. Acknowledge the user's concern
2. Provide a clear solution or next steps
3. Include a satisfaction survey link at the end

Effective constraint prompts are specific and unambiguous, leaving no room for interpretation. State constraints in terms of observable behavior rather than internal states.

Watch Out: Constraints Are Not Guarantees

A common misconception is that constraints in your prompt are always followed. The model will often follow constraints, but not always. A model might occasionally reveal a system prompt, fail to cite sources, or violate other constraints under certain conditions. For critical constraints, you need output guardrails (filters that check the final output) in addition to prompt constraints.

Combining Patterns

In practice, you rarely use just one pattern. The most effective prompts combine role prompting (establishing who you are), constraints (what you must and must not do), few-shot examples (how to format output), and sometimes chain-of-thought (how to reason). Building intuition for these combinations comes from iteration and evaluation.
