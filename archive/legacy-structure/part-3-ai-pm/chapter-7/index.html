Chapter 7

AI Product Strategy and Value Proposition Design

Defining Winning AI-Native Products

You have a working AI feature. Users engage with it, but they do not pay for it. The business case that justified the development does not hold up, and now you are defending a project that everyone agrees is technically impressive but commercially pointless.

This is the most common failure mode for AI products: solving a technical challenge that no one actually wants to pay for. The fix is strategic clarity from day one, and this chapter gives you the frameworks to achieve it.

Objective: Help define winning AI-native products with the right strategy and value proposition.

Chapter Overview

Building an AI product without strategy is like building a house without blueprints. You might end up with something standing, but it will not be where you wanted it to be. This chapter provides the strategic frameworks you need to define what kind of AI product to build, how to position it, and how to measure success in a world where outputs are probabilistic rather than deterministic. You will explore the AI product taxonomy to understand where your product fits, learn when to take a problem-first versus tech-first approach, master build/buy/bake decision frameworks, and develop value propositions that account for AI limitations while highlighting its unique strengths.

The strategic decisions you make early in your AI product journey have outsized impact on eventual success. Unlike traditional software where specifications can be precisely implemented, AI products require different thinking. The evaluation framework you choose, the metrics you track, and the value proposition you craft all need to account for the probabilistic nature of AI outputs. This chapter gives you the frameworks to make those decisions confidently.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the AI product taxonomy and where your product fits
  • Apply problem-first vs. tech-first thinking to product decisions
  • Use build/buy/bake frameworks for AI component decisions
  • Define success metrics for probabilistic AI features
  • Create value propositions that account for AI limitations

Prerequisites

This chapter builds on concepts from Chapter 1: AI Capabilities and Limitations. Understanding of basic product management concepts is helpful but not required. If you are new to product management, focus on understanding the problem-first approach in Section 7.2 and the value proposition frameworks in Section 7.4.
