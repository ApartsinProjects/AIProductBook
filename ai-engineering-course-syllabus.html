Comprehensive Production AI Curriculum

AI Engineering from Ground to Production

A complete, ground-up, project-based course for software engineers who want to design, build, evaluate, deploy, monitor, and scale modern AI systems in real products. The syllabus assumes only basic software engineering knowledge and progressively builds the full stack of practical AI engineering capabilities needed for production work.

Level: Beginner to Advanced Format: Hands-on + Demo-heavy Orientation: Production-first Scope: LLMs, agents, RAG, multimodal, MLOps

Who This Is For

  • Software engineers transitioning into AI engineering
  • Builders who can code but are new to modern AI systems
  • Teams that want one curriculum spanning prototypes to reliable production

By the End, Learners Will Be Able To

  • Choose the right model, architecture, and deployment pattern for a use case
  • Build robust RAG and agentic systems with tools, memory, and workflows
  • Ship production services with evals, observability, guardrails, and cost controls

Course Design Principles

The course is intentionally broad, modern, and practical. It covers not only model APIs, but also data, retrieval, orchestration, evaluation, reliability, compliance, performance, infrastructure, product thinking, and team workflows.

Ground Up

Starts with AI and LLM fundamentals, service patterns, APIs, JSON, embeddings, and vector search before moving into advanced systems.

Complete

Covers prompt and context engineering, RAG, agents, evaluation, safety, infra, CI/CD, MLOps, and operations.

Modern

Includes current practices such as reasoning models, tool use, MCP, multimodal pipelines, async workflows, tracing, and continuous evals.

Hands-On

Every module includes labs, demonstrations, design reviews, and portfolio-grade projects that compound into a final capstone.

Program At a Glance

A suggested 24-module sequence organized into six phases. The structure is suitable for a bootcamp, semester-length intensive, or self-paced cohort, with an explicit serving and production-systems spine that starts early and culminates in benchmark-driven inference work.

Phase 1: Foundations

Modules 1-4
AI basics, software stack, data formats, model APIs, prompting, embeddings, and the first introduction to latency and cost tradeoffs.

Phase 2: Core Systems

Modules 5-8
RAG pipelines, indexing, context engineering, structured outputs, workflows, async architecture, and retrieval quality.

Phase 3: Agentic Patterns

Modules 9-12
Tools, function calling, planners, memory, multi-step systems, MCP, connectors, and human-in-the-loop controls.

Phase 4: Productionization

Modules 13-16
Evaluation, testing, observability, safety, security, service-level metrics, and cost engineering.

Phase 5: Serving and Infrastructure

Modules 17-20
Deployment, LLM serving, GPUs, inference engines, pipelines, CI/CD, governance, and MLOps.

Phase 6: Specialization and Launch

Modules 21-24
Multimodal AI, realtime systems, fine-tuning, product strategy, capstone architecture, and launch readiness.

Built-In Background Bridge

The course assumes only basic software engineering knowledge, so the first phase explicitly teaches the missing foundations that many strong engineers have not formally studied in AI, ML, search, or production data systems.

Math and ML Basics

Probability, similarity intuition, precision and recall, train/dev/test thinking, and the language needed to discuss model behavior sensibly.

Software and Data Basics

HTTP, JSON, SQL, queues, webhooks, files, APIs, and the difference between application state, retrieval state, and model context.

Search and Retrieval Basics

Keyword search, BM25 intuition, embeddings, reranking, chunking, and when lexical methods outperform vector-only systems.

Production Basics

Logging, testing, deployment environments, cost awareness, latency awareness, and the idea that AI systems are software systems first.

Serving Spine

Rather than treating inference serving as a late infrastructure appendix, the course revisits serving decisions throughout the program so students learn to connect product behavior to latency, cost, architecture, and hardware realities.

Early Awareness

Modules 1-4 introduce tokens, latency, prompt shape, structured outputs, and why API choices affect downstream serving cost.

System Design

Modules 5-12 connect retrieval, workflows, tools, async execution, and agent design to serving load, responsiveness, and failure recovery.

Operational Metrics

Modules 13-16 formalize service-level engineering with TTFT, TPOT, throughput, goodput, SLOs, observability, and incident handling.

Inference Engineering

Modules 17-20 go deep on engines, KV cache, continuous batching, quantization, routing, autoscaling, and benchmark-driven serving decisions.

Detailed Module Syllabus

Each module includes theory, demonstration, guided build, and production perspective. The learner should leave every module with something that runs, can be tested, and can be discussed in design review terms.

1. What AI Engineers Actually Build

Orient learners to the AI product stack, common roles, system boundaries, failure modes, and the baseline concepts they will need later.

Foundation

Topics

  • AI engineer vs ML engineer vs data scientist vs platform engineer
  • Model-powered application anatomy: UI, service layer, orchestration, storage, observability
  • Use-case taxonomy: chat, extraction, automation, retrieval, copilots, agents, multimodal apps
  • Core prerequisite map: APIs, HTTP, JSON, SQL, cloud services, and debugging habits that matter in AI systems
  • Prototype versus production expectations

Lab

  • Map three real-world AI products to their underlying system components

2. AI, ML, LLM, and Deep Learning Fundamentals

Build intuition without requiring a research background, and supply the minimum math and evaluation language used later in the course.

Foundation

Topics

  • Supervised, unsupervised, self-supervised, and reinforcement learning
  • Neural networks, transformers, tokens, embeddings, attention, context windows
  • Pretraining, instruction tuning, RLHF and post-training concepts
  • Capabilities, limitations, hallucination, uncertainty, and brittleness
  • Probability and statistics basics for engineers: similarity, distributions, thresholds, precision, recall, and calibration
  • Classical ML intuition that still matters in AI products: classifiers, regression, overfitting, underfitting, and cross-validation

Lab

  • Tokenization and embedding visualization exercise with a short precision-versus-recall threshold analysis

3. AI Engineering Setup and Developer Workflow

Establish the practical stack and the missing runtime knowledge many software engineers need before building AI systems.

Foundation

Topics

  • Python and TypeScript service basics for AI apps, including common AI engineering libraries and notebooks-to-service workflows
  • Virtual environments, package managers, secrets, environment variables, Docker
  • REST APIs, HTTP fundamentals, JSON payloads, async jobs, webhooks, queues, background workers
  • SQL, NoSQL, object storage, files, logs, and the data stores commonly used in AI applications
  • Git, branching, code review, testing, reproducibility, notebooks versus services
  • AI-assisted coding workflows and best practices for tools such as Codex, Cursor, and Claude Code
  • Cloud and runtime basics: local development, containers, staging, production, and environment isolation

Lab

  • Create a local AI service skeleton with API, worker, storage, and config layers

4. Prompt Engineering and Structured Outputs

Move beyond ad hoc prompting into repeatable interface design.

Core

Topics

  • Instructions, roles, examples, delimiters, decomposition, verification prompts
  • Schema-constrained outputs, JSON mode, tool invocation contracts
  • Reliability patterns: retries, validators, fallback prompts, self-checks
  • Prompt versioning, prompt asset management, and token-budget awareness

Lab

  • Build a structured information extraction pipeline with validation

5. Search Foundations, Embeddings, and Vector Databases

Teach the foundation of retrieval-driven AI systems without assuming prior search-engine knowledge.

Core

Topics

  • Keyword search, lexical retrieval, BM25 intuition, embeddings, distance metrics, chunking, metadata, hybrid retrieval
  • Vector stores, indexing tradeoffs, filters, freshness, re-indexing strategies
  • Precision, recall, grounding, semantic drift, and when lexical methods beat vector-only methods
  • When not to use a vector database

Lab

  • Build keyword, vector, and hybrid search over a document corpus and compare chunking and ranking strategies

6. Retrieval-Augmented Generation End to End

Build production RAG pipelines that answer accurately and cite sources.

Core

Topics

  • Ingestion, parsing, cleaning, chunking, indexing, retrieval, ranking, generation
  • Source attribution, answer synthesis, citation UX, abstention behavior
  • Offline and online evaluation for retrieval and answer quality
  • Failure patterns in enterprise knowledge systems

Lab

  • Ship a document question-answering service with citations and answer confidence policy

7. Context Engineering and Memory Design

Teach learners how to control what the model sees and remembers.

Core

Topics

  • Conversation state, session memory, summaries, retrieval memory, profile memory
  • Context windows, caching, compression, salience selection, truncation policies
  • Short-term versus long-term memory
  • Privacy and data retention implications of stateful systems

Lab

  • Implement memory policies for a support copilot and compare behavior over long sessions

8. Workflow-Oriented AI Applications

Show how many useful AI systems are workflows, not autonomous agents.

Core

Topics

  • Classification, routing, extraction, transformation, summarization, generation pipelines
  • Deterministic orchestration around stochastic model calls
  • State machines, DAGs, job queues, retries, dead-letter handling, resumability
  • Background mode, long-running jobs, webhooks, callback handling, and user notification patterns
  • Online inference versus batch and flex processing for enrichment, backfills, and eval pipelines
  • When to combine LLMs with classical ML, rules, or heuristics instead of using an LLM end to end
  • Human approval checkpoints

Lab

  • Build an intake triage system that classifies, extracts, routes work, and completes asynchronously through queues and webhooks

9. Tool Use, Function Calling, and External Actions

Teach safe and dependable connections between models and real systems.

Agentic

Topics

  • Tool definitions, argument schemas, tool selection, retries, idempotency
  • Search, database, code, email, ticketing, browser, computer-use, and business-action tools
  • Permissioning, auditability, rollback, side-effect management, and sandbox design
  • OAuth scopes, enterprise connectors, SSO-aware integrations, and approval boundaries for external actions
  • Toolformer-style thinking versus bounded orchestration

Lab

  • Create an AI assistant that queries systems, uses a controlled browser/computer-use tool, and drafts actions without unsafe execution

10. Agent Architectures and Planning Patterns

Separate useful agent patterns from hype and over-engineering.

Agentic

Topics

  • Single-agent loops, planner-executor, critic, router, swarm, hierarchical, and agent-to-agent collaboration patterns
  • When to use workflows instead of agents
  • Task decomposition, reflection, structured planning, interruption and recovery
  • Latency, cost, determinism, and product tradeoffs
  • Evaluating agent actions, tool correctness, task completion, and approval compliance

Lab

  • Compare three architectures for the same operations automation task, including a workflow-first design, a bounded agent, and a multi-agent handoff design

11. MCP and AI System Interoperability

Introduce modern standards and interfaces for tool and context connectivity.

Agentic

Topics

  • Model Context Protocol concepts and ecosystem role
  • Tool servers, documentation servers, knowledge connectors, local vs remote integration
  • Standardized interfaces versus custom adapters and emerging inter-agent interoperability patterns
  • Security boundaries, trust models, approvals, OAuth flows, and remote connector governance
  • Third-party MCP risk review, least-privilege design, and tenant-aware data access

Lab

  • Connect an assistant to controlled local and remote tool/context servers and document the trust, auth, and approval model

12. Building Human-in-the-Loop Systems

Teach systems that are useful, governable, and aligned with real work.

Agentic

Topics

  • Approval workflows, escalation paths, review queues, exception handling
  • Operator UX, transparency, explainability, and reversible actions
  • Designing for trust and calibrated reliance
  • Feedback capture for future evaluation and improvement

Lab

  • Build a review-and-approve workflow for AI-generated actions in a business process

13. AI Evaluation and Quality Engineering

Make learners fluent in evals as a central production discipline.

Production

Topics

  • Golden datasets, task success metrics, rubric evals, model-graded evals, human review, and online measurement
  • Regression testing, scenario coverage, adversarial testing, red teaming
  • Retrieval evals versus generation evals versus action-level and workflow-level evals
  • Eval dataset operations: curation, annotation, synthetic generation, failure bucketing, versioning, and collaboration with SMEs or labelers
  • Prompt optimization loops with graders, test sets, and controlled prompt revisions
  • Foundational metrics review: accuracy, precision, recall, F1, ranking quality, thresholding, and tradeoff interpretation
  • Continuous evaluation in CI/CD and post-deployment

Lab

  • Create an eval harness for a RAG or agent system, including dataset versioning, grader prompts, action checks, and CI gates

14. Observability, Tracing, and Production Debugging

Give learners the tools to understand live AI systems and diagnose failures.

Production

Topics

  • Request tracing, prompt and response logging, tool-call traces, retrieval traces
  • Token, latency, quality, cost, cache-hit, and tool-success telemetry
  • PII-safe logging, sampling, aggregation, dashboards, and tenant-aware tracing
  • Using observability to improve prompts, retrieval, caches, and workflows
  • AI-specific incident triage for hallucinations, prompt injection, connector faults, provider outages, and runaway spend

Lab

  • Instrument an AI service with end-to-end traces, cache metrics, and an incident-focused debugging dashboard

15. Safety, Security, and Responsible AI

Treat risk management as core engineering, not an appendix.

Production

Topics

  • Prompt injection, data exfiltration, insecure tool access, jailbreaks, model misuse, and browser/computer-use abuse
  • PII handling, retention, compliance-aware design, audit logs, and tenant isolation
  • Content safety, abuse prevention, policy enforcement, fallback behavior
  • Threat modeling for RAG, agentic, connector-based, and enterprise-integrated systems

Lab

  • Red-team an agent system and add layered mitigations

16. Cost, Latency, Reliability, and Service-Level Engineering

Help learners operate AI products within real business constraints and measurable service targets.

Production

Topics

  • Model selection by task, cascade architectures, prompt caching, semantic caching, batching, streaming
  • Synchronous vs background execution, queue-based designs, graceful degradation, and offline batch processing
  • Rate limits, retries, fallback models, circuit breakers, SLAs, SLOs, and error budgets
  • Serving metrics such as time-to-first-token, time-per-output-token, throughput, goodput, and tail latency
  • Cost attribution, budget controls, cache-aware prompt design, and gateway-level routing policies

Lab

  • Optimize an AI endpoint for quality, latency, and cost under a fixed budget using prompt caching, response reuse, model routing, and service-level metrics

17. Serving AI Applications in Production

Move from local demos to deployed, supportable systems.

Infrastructure

Topics

  • Service design, API gateways, auth, configuration, tenancy, secret management, and LLM gateways
  • Cloud deployment patterns, containers, serverless, worker pools, edge runtimes, and mobile/web client considerations
  • Online inference versus batch jobs, async callbacks, event-driven architectures, and streaming transport choices
  • Orchestration layer versus inference layer, request routing, load balancing, and warm-pool strategies
  • Blue-green deployments, canaries, rollback plans

Lab

  • Deploy an AI app with separate API, worker, gateway, inference, and monitoring services, plus a streaming client experience

18. LLM Serving Systems, Inference Engines, and Self-Hosted Models

Give practical understanding of modern LLM serving as a systems discipline, not just deployment.

Infrastructure

Topics

  • Inference servers and frameworks: vLLM, TGI, TensorRT-LLM, llama.cpp, managed endpoints, and OpenAI-compatible serving
  • Quantization, throughput, batch sizing, concurrency, continuous batching, and KV-cache behavior
  • Paged attention, memory fragmentation, prefix caching, chunked prefill, and prefill-versus-decode optimization
  • GPU basics, VRAM planning, autoscaling challenges, cold starts, and throughput benchmarking
  • Hosted APIs versus open-weight deployment across single-GPU, multi-GPU, and multi-node serving tiers
  • Latency, privacy, sovereignty, economics tradeoffs, and speculative decoding performance tactics
  • Serving reasoning models, long-context models, multimodal models, and model-specific serving constraints

Lab

  • Benchmark a hosted model against a self-hosted open model and compare two serving frameworks using TTFT, TPOT, throughput, goodput, batching, and decode-strategy tradeoffs

19. Data Pipelines, Governance, and AI MLOps

Connect application engineering to data and operational discipline.

Infrastructure

Topics

  • Data ingestion pipelines, labeling, feedback loops, versioning, lineage, and eval-data pipelines
  • Feature/data quality concepts for AI applications, feature engineering intuition, and document-ingestion quality control
  • Modern data stack exposure: Kafka, Airflow, Snowflake, warehouses, and distributed data workflows
  • Experiment tracking, model and prompt registries, release management, and personalization assets
  • Governance for enterprise knowledge, training data, user profiles, and memory stores

Lab

  • Create a pipeline that captures feedback, buckets failures, and turns them into eval, personalization, or training assets

20. Testing, CI/CD, and Team Delivery Practices

Train learners to ship AI systems with engineering rigor.

Infrastructure

Topics

  • Unit, integration, end-to-end, contract, online experiment, and evaluation tests for AI systems
  • Replay testing, mock providers, fixture management, deterministic wrappers, and action simulation harnesses
  • CI/CD for prompts, configs, retrieval indexes, and service code
  • Release notes, operational checklists, incident response, and AI-specific rollback criteria

Lab

  • Build a release pipeline for an AI service including tests, evals, rollout gates, and an A/B or canary experiment plan

21. Multimodal AI Systems

Expand learners beyond text into modern production AI interfaces.

Specialization

Topics

  • Image, audio, document, and vision-language workflows
  • OCR, transcription, image understanding, screenshot analysis, voice interfaces, and realtime speech systems
  • Multimodal retrieval, layout-aware chunking, and document intelligence ingestion pipelines
  • Streaming UX, turn-taking, interruption handling, and UX implications of multimodal systems

Lab

  • Build a document intake or voice-enabled multimodal system that extracts, streams, and reasons over text, images, and audio

22. Fine-Tuning, Adaptation, and Customization

Teach when customization is worth the effort and when it is not.

Specialization

Topics

  • Prompting versus retrieval versus fine-tuning versus distillation
  • Dataset design, quality control, evaluation before and after tuning
  • Specialized models for classification, extraction, or style adaptation
  • Risk, maintenance cost, and governance of custom models

Lab

  • Run a small adaptation experiment and compare it against prompt-only and RAG baselines

23. Product Strategy for AI Features

Make learners strong at deciding what should be built, not only how.

Specialization

Topics

  • Use-case selection, ROI framing, user trust, UX for uncertainty, approval, and personalized experiences
  • Build vs buy decisions, vendor risk, platform lock-in, pricing strategies, and gateway/provider abstraction
  • Success metrics, rollout strategy, adoption, A/B testing, online experiments, and change management
  • Stakeholder discovery, workshop facilitation, requirements gathering, and translating ambiguous business goals into technical plans
  • Writing AI design docs, reference implementations, technical guides, and production readiness reviews

Lab

  • Produce a design review memo and technical rollout guide for a new AI feature in an existing product, including experiment plan, personalization policy, and provider strategy

24. Capstone Build and Launch Readiness

Integrate the whole course into a final, production-quality system.

Capstone

Topics

  • Architecture review, security review, eval review, observability review
  • Performance testing, launch checklist, runbooks, incident playbooks
  • Stakeholder demo and technical walkthrough
  • Post-launch improvement roadmap

Lab

  • Launch the final project with a deployment plan, dashboard, and test evidence

Portfolio Projects

The course is anchored by cumulative projects so learners graduate with tangible, job-relevant work instead of isolated exercises.

Project 1: AI Support Copilot

  • Multi-turn assistant for support agents
  • RAG over policy and product docs with reranking and query rewrite
  • Structured response templates, citations, and personalization boundaries
  • Eval suite for answer quality, hallucination rate, and retrieval quality

Project 2: AI Workflow Automation Service

  • Inbox or ticket triage pipeline
  • Extraction, classification, routing, approval, async completion, and hybrid ML or rules where appropriate
  • Human-in-the-loop dashboard with webhook and queue status
  • Queue-based architecture with retries, audit logs, and incident runbook

Project 3: Agent with Tools

  • Bounded agent that searches data, uses connectors, and drafts actions
  • Tool permission model, side-effect controls, and browser/computer-use sandboxing
  • Tracing, red-team tests, action-level evals, and fallback behavior
  • Operational review of cost, latency, gateway routing, and safety

Project 4: Multimodal Document Intelligence App

  • Processes PDFs, screenshots, forms, invoices, or voice inputs
  • OCR, extraction, layout-aware ingestion, validation, and review workflow
  • Confidence thresholds, exception queues, and streaming or realtime UX
  • Production dashboard for throughput, ingestion quality, and accuracy

Project 5: Self-Hosted vs API Benchmark

  • Compare hosted and open-weight deployments across two serving stacks
  • Measure quality, cost, TTFT, TPOT, throughput, goodput, privacy, and ops burden
  • Write architecture recommendation memo including caching, routing, and serving-framework strategy
  • Present tradeoff-driven decision to a stakeholder panel

Final Capstone: Production AI Product

  • Student-defined use case with clear business value
  • Live deployment, observability, tests, evals, experiment plan, and stakeholder-facing documentation
  • Security, connector trust, and reliability review
  • Launch demo plus post-launch roadmap for iteration and incident handling

Guiding philosophy: the strongest AI engineers are not the ones who can merely call a model API. They can shape ambiguous problems, choose the right level of autonomy, design for failure, evaluate quality, protect users and data, and operate systems responsibly after launch.

Suggested Delivery Format

The syllabus is flexible, but one effective implementation is a 24-week program with one main module per week, one lab, and one project milestone every two to three weeks. A strong delivery pattern is to pair each week’s application topic with a short production-systems clinic so serving and operations stay visible throughout.

Weekly Rhythm

  • Concept lecture and architecture discussion
  • Live build or debugging demonstration
  • Production-systems clinic on serving, evals, or operations
  • Hands-on lab session
  • Project office hours and review

Artifacts Students Produce

  • Working repositories
  • Architecture diagrams and design notes
  • Evaluation reports, serving benchmarks, and dashboards
  • Deployment checklists, launch plans, and performance recommendation memos

Capstone Exit Standard

  • Deployed service or documented local production simulation
  • Evidence of tests, evals, observability, and serving benchmarks
  • Security and risk review
  • Business-value narrative and improvement roadmap
