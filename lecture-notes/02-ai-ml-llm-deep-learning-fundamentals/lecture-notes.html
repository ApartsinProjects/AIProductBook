AI, ML, LLM, and Deep Learning Fundamentals

This module gives students the mental model behind the tools they will use for the rest of the course. The goal is not to turn them into ML researchers. The goal is to make them fluent enough to reason about models, data, uncertainty, embeddings, transformers, thresholds, and evaluation tradeoffs in production AI systems.

How To Read This Chapter

This chapter narrows the lens from Module 1. There, students learned to view AI products as systems. Here, they learn just enough model intuition to reason sensibly about those systems: what the major terms mean, how modern language models represent text, where capability comes from, and why evaluation language such as precision, recall, and threshold choice matters in product work.

It builds directly on Module 1 mental models. Companion materials: lab, lab solution, and slides.

Prerequisites and scope: students only need the Module 1 system view plus basic software engineering. No calculus-heavy ML background is assumed. The point is not to make students ML researchers. It is to give them enough internal model understanding that later topics like prompting, retrieval, evaluation, and serving feel conceptually grounded instead of magical.

Models Are Statistical Systems

This chapter moves from category map, to learning signal, to representations, to training, to evaluation. The main theme is that AI engineers do not need every mathematical detail, but they do need accurate intuitions about where model behavior comes from and how it affects system design.

2. Landscape

AI, ML, Deep Learning, Transformers, and LLMs Are Nested Concepts

One of the first confusions students have is category confusion. They hear “AI” and “LLM” used as if they are the same thing. They are related, but they sit at different levels of abstraction.

Artificial Intelligence

Any technique intended to produce useful intelligent behavior. This is the broadest term.

Machine Learning

Systems that learn patterns from data rather than relying only on hand-written rules.

Deep Learning

Machine learning based on neural networks with many layers and learned internal representations.

Transformers And LLMs

Transformers are a model architecture. LLMs are large transformer-based language models trained on massive text corpora.

Category Comparison

TermWhat it meansWhy an AI engineer should care
AIBroad umbrella for intelligent systemsHelps place products in the wider space of automation and reasoning
MLLearning from examples and dataExplains why data quality and labels matter
Deep learningRepresentation learning with neural networksExplains why models can learn flexible patterns from raw inputs
TransformerArchitecture built around attentionExplains tokens, context windows, scaling, and modern model behavior
LLMLarge pretrained language modelExplains why prompting, context engineering, and post-training matter in apps

A useful way to read this table is as a nesting guide. “AI” tells you the broad problem family. “ML” tells you that the system learns from data. “Deep learning” tells you that internal representations matter. “Transformer” tells you which architecture family is shaping context use. “LLM” tells you which application pattern is now possible at scale. Each lower row narrows the engineering conversation.

Connection forward: once students understand the landscape, the next question is how these systems actually learn. That means distinguishing supervised, unsupervised, self-supervised, and reinforcement learning.
3. Learning Paradigms

How Models Learn: The Big Families

Engineers do not need to derive the algorithms from scratch, but they do need to understand what type of learning signal a model gets. That tells us what a model is likely to be good at and where its blind spots come from.

Supervised Learning

Learn from labeled examples: input -> desired output.

Unsupervised Learning

Find structure without explicit labels: clusters, latent structure, compression.

Self-Supervised Learning

Create supervision from the data itself, such as predicting missing or next tokens.

Reinforcement Learning

Learn by interacting with an environment and receiving rewards or penalties. For AI engineers, this matters mostly as background for post-training ideas and agent behavior, not because every production application uses RL directly.

Most Important Practical Insight

LLMs are not usually trained by feeding in millions of perfect human question-answer pairs at the start. Their initial general capability mainly comes from self-supervised learning on large corpora, then later post-training makes them more helpful and aligned for user-facing work.

Illustration: What Signal The Model Receives

Original diagram for this course. The point is to anchor the intuition around learning signal rather than formula details.

Common misconception to explicitly correct

“LLMs are just supervised models trained to answer questions.” That misses the core story. Their broad language competence mainly emerges from self-supervised pretraining; supervised and preference-style post-training shape usefulness after that.

4. Neural Networks

Why Neural Networks Matter

Traditional ML often relies on manual features or relatively shallow decision boundaries. Neural networks matter because they can learn their own internal representations. That turns many hard perception and language tasks from manual feature engineering problems into representation learning problems.

Large-Picture Intuition

A neural network is a stack of functions that gradually transforms raw input into a space where the final prediction becomes easier. Early layers might detect simple patterns; later layers combine them into more abstract concepts.

Why This Matters For AI Engineers

This is why embeddings, hidden states, and model representations are so important later. Models are not just memorizing answers. They are building internal spaces where semantic similarity and useful distinctions can be represented.

Illustration: Representation Learning

Original diagram for this course. Each layer is not magic; it is a learned transformation that makes the final problem easier.

Classical ML Intuition That Still Matters

ConceptHigh-level meaningWhy it still matters in AI products
ClassifierPick a categoryRouting, moderation, triage, document tagging, guardrails

This table is here to keep students from treating LLMs as a complete break from earlier ML. The vocabulary of classifiers, regression, overfitting, and generalization still matters because AI products are still being judged by whether they transfer from examples to real operating conditions. A slick demo can still fail for the same old reasons.

Connection backward: Module 1 emphasized that product quality is shaped by task framing. Neural nets add another layer to that story: the model learns internal patterns, but good product framing still decides what the model should and should not do.
5. Transformers

Why Transformers Changed Modern AI Engineering

Transformers matter because they made it practical to model long-range relationships in sequences while training in parallel at scale. For engineers, the important intuition is simpler: transformers let modern language models use token-level context much more effectively than earlier generations.

Attention Intuition

When interpreting a token, the model can weight other tokens by relevance. That means the representation of a word or symbol is not fixed in isolation. It changes depending on context.

Why Engineers Care

This is the root of many later concepts: context windows, prompt construction, retrieval augmentation, token budgets, and why prompt order or surrounding evidence can drastically change output behavior.

Illustration: Context Changes Meaning

Original diagram for this course. Same token string, different contextual meaning. This is one of the core intuitions behind attention-based models.

Deep insight: prompt engineering is really context engineering

Students often think prompts are magic spells. A better mental model is that prompt design is one specific case of context design. You are shaping which information the model can attend to and how that information is structured.

6. Tokens And Embeddings

Tokens Are The Model’s Basic Units, Embeddings Are Its Basic Representations

Tokenization and embeddings are among the most practical concepts in the whole course. They directly affect cost, context limits, similarity search, chunking strategy, and retrieval system behavior.

Token Intuition

A token is a chunk of text the model processes as one unit. Tokens are not the same thing as words.

Embedding Intuition

An embedding is a vector representation where semantically related inputs tend to end up closer together.

7. Training Lifecycle

From Pretraining To Post-Training To Inference

Students should understand model lifecycle at a high level, because it explains both strengths and limitations. Why can a model write fluent text? Why can it still hallucinate? Why does instruction tuning help? Why does RLHF change style more than raw world knowledge?

Illustration: The Modern LLM Lifecycle

Original diagram for this course. The key lesson is that broad language capability and user-facing helpfulness come from different stages.

Pretraining

The model learns broad language and pattern knowledge by predicting text from large corpora. This stage creates general competence.

Instruction Tuning And Preference Training

These stages shape the model to follow instructions, be more helpful, safer, and better aligned with human preferences. They usually change interaction quality more than basic world knowledge.

Common misconception to explicitly correct

“RLHF makes the model smart.” More precisely, it often makes the model more useful, more compliant with desired conversational behavior, and better aligned with human judgments. Raw competence and post-training behavior are related but distinct.

8. Metrics And Thresholds

Why Precision, Recall, Thresholds, And Calibration Matter So Much

This is one of the most practically valuable sections for AI engineers. Many real systems do not fail because the model architecture is wrong. They fail because teams choose the wrong threshold, optimize the wrong metric, or ignore confidence calibration.

Precision

Of the things the model predicted positive, how many were actually right?

Recall

Of the things that really were positive, how many did the model catch?

Calibration

Does the model’s confidence roughly match real-world accuracy?

Illustration: Threshold Choice Changes Product Behavior

Original diagram for this course. One threshold does not fit every product; the cost of false positives and false negatives changes the right choice.

Metric Choice By Product Scenario

ScenarioMetric instinctWhy
Fraud flagging for reviewHigher recall may matter moreMissing a true fraud case may be more costly than reviewing extra cases
Auto-send customer messagesHigher precision may matter moreFalse positives can create bad user-facing behavior
Human review queue routingCalibration mattersYou need confidence scores that mean something operationally

Deep insight: metrics are really about costs

Precision and recall are not abstract exam concepts. They are a way of expressing business and product tradeoffs. A false positive and a false negative have different costs in different systems. Strong AI engineers map metrics back to those costs.

9. Capabilities, Limits, And Failures

Why Powerful Models Are Still Brittle

Students should leave this module respecting both model capability and model fragility. Over-hyping and underestimating are both dangerous. The right stance is operational realism.

Hallucination

The model generates plausible text that is not grounded in fact.

Brittleness

Small phrasing or context changes can produce large behavior shifts.

Uncertainty

Confidence is often implicit and not perfectly calibrated.

Why These Failures Happen

  • Models are optimizing prediction, not guaranteed truth retrieval
  • Training data is imperfect and broad, not task-specific in every case
  • Context windows are limited and selective
  • Inference-time prompts cannot fully replace missing world knowledge or missing evidence

Why Engineers Should Care

  • This is why retrieval matters later
  • This is why structured outputs and validators matter later
  • This is why evals and observability matter later
  • This is why product trust must be designed instead of assumed
Connection backward: Module 1 said a wrong answer can come from many layers, not only the prompt. This module explains the model-layer reasons why even a well-designed system can remain probabilistic and brittle.
10. Examples

What The Fundamentals Look Like In Practice

Fundamentals are easiest to remember when students see them embodied in code, metrics, and product decisions rather than only in definitions.

Example 1: Token Counting Matters

text = "Tokenization matters because cost and context windows matter."

tokens = tokenize(text)
print(len(tokens))

# Engineering implication:
# fewer tokens can reduce cost and latency,
# but over-compressing can remove needed context.

This is the first glimpse of why prompt shape, chunking, and later latency engineering matter.

Example 2: Embedding Similarity

sentences = [
    "reset my password",
    "I can't sign in to my account",
    "how to cook pasta"
]

vectors = embed(sentences)
sim_1 = cosine_similarity(vectors[0], vectors[1])
sim_2 = cosine_similarity(vectors[0], vectors[2])

print(sim_1, sim_2)

This is the core intuition behind semantic search: useful closeness in vector space can represent meaning rather than exact string overlap.

Example 3: Threshold Tuning

scores = [0.91, 0.82, 0.63, 0.57, 0.42]
threshold = 0.80

predicted_positive = [s for s in scores if s >= threshold]
print(predicted_positive)

# Lower threshold -> more positives -> higher recall, lower precision
# Higher threshold -> fewer positives -> higher precision, lower recall

This simple pattern appears everywhere in production triage and automation systems.

Example 4: Better Mental Framing

Weak framing:
"Use AI to decide customer support actions."

Better framing:
1. Classify issue type
2. Estimate urgency score
3. Retrieve policy excerpts
4. Draft a reply with citations
5. Route to review if confidence is below threshold

This builds directly on Module 1’s task-framing lesson: fundamentals become useful when they help engineers decompose real systems into measurable, testable subproblems.
