# Slide Design Rubric And Checklist

Use this rubric to review teaching slides for this course, especially when the deck must work both for live teaching and for self-study.

## 1. Learning Design

- Does the deck clearly support specific learning outcomes?
- Does it start from the large picture before details?
- Does the sequence feel teachable, not just informative?
- Does each slide do one main job?
- Does the deck build understanding progressively rather than dumping information?
- Do slides connect to prior concepts and future modules where needed?

## 2. Self-Contained Usefulness

- If a student opens one slide later, can they understand its main point?
- Does each slide have a clear takeaway title?
- Are cross-references present where previously introduced concepts are used?
- Are notes/lab/solution links present where useful?
- Is there enough context to revisit the deck without the instructor present?
- Is the deck still concise enough for live teaching?

## 3. Cognitive Load

- Is there too much text on any slide?
- Could a student understand the slide in a quick glance?
- Are bullet lists short enough to scan easily?
- Are dense explanations moved to notes, companion documents, or later slides?
- Are visual and textual elements aligned, rather than competing for attention?
- Does the slide avoid making students read long paragraphs while listening?

## 4. Typography And Readability

- Are title fonts large and commanding?
- Are body fonts large enough for projection and room viewing?
- Is the line spacing comfortable?
- Is the text hierarchy obvious at a glance?
- Are long lines avoided where possible?
- Are fonts readable and visually consistent?

## 5. Layout And Screen Use

- Is the screen estate used efficiently?
- Are slides too empty or too crowded?
- Do diagrams have enough room to breathe?
- Are cards, columns, and comparison blocks balanced?
- Are margins and spacing consistent?
- Does the layout support fast comprehension?

## 6. Visual Explanation

- Does the deck use diagrams where they improve understanding?
- Are diagrams pedagogically useful rather than decorative?
- Is there at least one strong “big map” or conceptual overview?
- Are architecture/process/flow visuals used where helpful?
- Are diagrams large enough to read on screen?
- Are repeated visual motifs used to reinforce continuity?

## 7. Style And Polish

- Does the deck feel like a presentation rather than a document pasted into slides?
- Is the visual identity coherent across the deck?
- Are colors intentional and readable?
- Are there too many visual styles competing on screen?
- Do callouts and quote boxes genuinely help?
- Does the deck feel professional and deliberate?

## 8. Accessibility

- Is contrast strong enough for projected use?
- Is meaning conveyed by more than color alone?
- Are links readable and descriptive?
- Are slides still understandable in grayscale or for color-blind viewers?
- Is motion minimal and non-distracting?
- Are controls/navigation visible and understandable?

## 9. Presentation Practicality

- Can the instructor speak naturally without reading from the slide?
- Do the slides support live explanation rather than replace it?
- Is there a reasonable amount of content per slide for a live class?
- Is the deck easy to navigate in front of an audience?
- Is there an overview or jump mode for non-linear teaching?

## 10. Scoring Guide

Use this optional scoring scale:

- `5` = excellent, presentation-ready
- `4` = strong, minor polish opportunities
- `3` = usable, noticeable design or teaching issues
- `2` = weak, needs redesign in important areas
- `1` = poor, not suitable for strong teaching use

Recommended overall judgment:

- `4.5-5.0`: excellent deck
- `4.0-4.4`: strong deck
- `3.5-3.9`: solid but should get another polish pass
- `<3.5`: needs meaningful redesign

## 11. Fast QA Pass

Before finalizing a deck, ask:

- Can the main idea of each slide be understood in 5 seconds?
- Is the font large enough for the back of the room?
- Is there any slide that should obviously be split into two?
- Is there any slide that feels like notes rather than slides?
- Are the visuals doing real teaching work?
- Does the deck feel self-contained without becoming overcrowded?
