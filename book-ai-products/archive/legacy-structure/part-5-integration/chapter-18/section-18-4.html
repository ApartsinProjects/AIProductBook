18.4 What Can Go Wrong: Lessons from High-Profile Failures

The AI product landscape is littered with products that seemed promising but failed to deliver. Examining these failures reveals patterns that can help you avoid the same mistakes. This section analyzes three notable case studies.

Why Study Failures?

Imagine learning to drive by only studying car races where everything went perfectly. You'd have no idea what to do when it rains, when a tire blows, or when another driver makes a mistake. Similarly, studying AI failures teaches you what to watch for - cost overruns, trust issues, and product-market misalignments that aren't obvious from success stories.

Fun Note: The MVP That Cost $10 Million

A startup spent 18 months building an AI product, then discovered users wanted something completely different. The entire $10M investment was for the wrong product. The lesson: user research before engineering would have cost $50,000 and 2 months. MVP stands for Minimum Viable Product, not Maximum Viable Product.

Why This Matters

Learning from others' failures is cheaper than learning from your own. Understanding why high-profile AI products failed gives you a checklist of things to watch for in your own product development.

Case Study 1: OpenAI Sora

What Happened

Sora was announced as a breakthrough in video generation, producing stunning one-minute videos from text prompts. However, by late 2024, it became clear that the product faced significant challenges.

What Went Wrong

Three Critical Problems
  • High Inference Cost: Each video cost $X in compute, making consumer pricing unsustainable. At scale, daily compute costs would exceed revenue.
  • The Novelty Cliff: Initial demos impressed, but users found the experience wore off quickly. The "wow factor" didn't translate to retention.
  • Quality Moat Erosion: Within 3-6 months, competitors (Runway, Pika, Kling) caught up, diminishing the differentiation advantage.

Why This Matters for Your Product

Skill Demonstration: Cost-Before-Building
// Calculate cost per engagement before building
function calculateCostPerEngagement() {
  const modelCost = 0.03; // per minute of video
  const computeOverhead = 0.02; // infrastructure
  const totalCost = modelCost + computeOverhead;
  
  // At what price point is this sustainable?
  // Consumer: $9.99/month, 5 videos/month = $2/video
  // Sora's cost: $0.05/video -> sustainable
  // But what about 50 videos/month? 
  // Cost: $2.50, revenue: $2.00 -> lose money
  
  // Lesson: Calculate sustainable usage, not just base cost
  return {
    costPerVideo: totalCost,
    breakEvenAtMonthlyVideos: totalCost / 2, // assuming $2 avg revenue
    conclusion: 'Need usage limits or B2B model'
  };
}

Case Study 2: Klarna AI Assistant

What Happened

Klarna launched an AI assistant to handle customer service queries, claiming it would replace human Agents. Early results showed high automation, but problems emerged.

What Went Wrong

The Full Replacement Fallacy
  • Complex Issue Resolution: AI handled simple queries well but failed on complex issues like fraud disputes, chargebacks, and account takeovers. These required human judgment.
  • Trust Erosion: Some users felt "shunted" to AI and escalated faster, increasing support volume instead of decreasing it.
  • Nuance Failures: Financial services require precise language. AI occasionally gave imprecise answers that created legal exposure.
Real-World Scenario: The Chargeback Scenario

A customer disputed a $200 charge. The AI assistant processed the dispute correctly but missed a key detail: this was the customer's third dispute in 30 days, suggesting potential fraud. A human agent would have flagged this pattern and initiated additional verification. The AI just processed the standard workflow. Result: legitimate chargeback, loss of $200 + investigation cost = $250 total loss from one missed edge case.

Case Study 3: Humane AI Pin

What Happened

Humane launched a $700 wearable AI device as a "phone replacement." Despite significant investment and hype, sales were disappointing and the company was eventually acquired for a fraction of its valuation.

What Went Wrong

Product-Market Fit Failure
  • No Clear Use Case: What would you do with it that your phone doesn't do better? The answer was unclear, and the product required learning new interaction patterns.
  • Performance Issues: Latency was higher than expected, voice recognition struggled in noisy environments, and the projector was hard to read outdoors.
  • Price Mismatch: At $700 + $24/month subscription, it needed to be dramatically better than a free phone app. It wasn't.
  • Integration Gap: The product existed in isolation rather than enhancing existing workflows.
Real-World Scenario: The "Show Me My Order" Test

A common use case: "What's my order status?" On phone: 3 taps, 2 seconds. On AI Pin: Tap device, say "order status," wait for response, 5+ seconds. The AI Pin was slower than the existing solution for every real task. This fundamental issue - AI was adding friction rather than removing it - was the root cause of failure.

Key Lessons Summary

Critical Lessons
  • Calculate model inference cost per engagement before setting pricing - You need sustainable unit economics from day one.
  • Novelty is not a retention strategy - Users need ongoing value, not just initial excitement.
  • Quality moats erode in 3-6 months - Plan for competition and differentiation that goes beyond model capability.
  • Hybrid > full replacement - AI-assisted humans outperform either pure AI or pure human approaches.
  • Product-market fit is non-negotiable - The best technology fails if it doesn't solve a clear user need better than alternatives.

Prevention Checklist

Before Launch, Verify:

COST SUSTAINABILITY
□ Cost per engagement calculated
□ Pricing covers costs at expected scale
□ Contingency for usage spikes

RETENTION PLAN
□ Beyond novelty, what's the ongoing value?
□ What's the frequency of use?
□ What's the switching cost from alternatives?

COMPETITIVE POSITIONING  
□ What's the moat beyond model quality?
□ How quickly could competitors replicate?
□ What prevents "just use ChatGPT"?

APPROACH REALISM
□ Is hybrid model viable for this use case?
□ Can edge cases be identified and escalated?
□ What's the error tolerance for this domain?

USER NEED
□ Would users pay for this vs. free alternatives?
□ Is this faster/simpler than existing solutions?
□ What's the actual use case, not the theoretical one?
Key Takeaways
  • High inference costs can make products unsustainable
  • Novelty wears off - plan for long-term value
  • Quality moats erode fast - build additional differentiation
  • Hybrid models outperform full replacement
  • Product-market fit matters more than technology
  • Use the prevention checklist before launch
