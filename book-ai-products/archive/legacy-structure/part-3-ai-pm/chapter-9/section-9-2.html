8.2 Prioritization with Kill Criteria

Objective: Handle uncertainty in AI products through adapted research, prioritization, and data strategies.

Prerequisites: Completion of Section 8.1 recommended.
Big Picture: Setting Boundaries for AI Deployment

Kill criteria define explicit thresholds that protect users and businesses from AI products that are not ready for production. The fundamental challenge is that limited testing cannot reveal all failure modes. Defining kill criteria before launch transforms subjective "good enough" assessments into objective go/no-go decisions based on predetermined thresholds for accuracy, hallucination rates, response time, and cost per interaction.

Fun Note: The Kill Switch Kings

Russian spacecraft have had physical kill switches for over 50 years. The reason: engineers learned that no matter how much you test, something will always go wrong. The best you can do is define when to pull the plug.

8.2 Prioritization with Kill Criteria

Kill criteria are explicit thresholds that determine whether an AI feature should be launched or discontinued. They are not failure conditions to be avoided but rather predetermined boundaries that protect users and the business from AI products that are not ready. Defining kill criteria before launch is essential because AI products can appear to work in limited testing while failing in production.

Why Kill Criteria Matter

The fundamental challenge with AI products is that limited testing cannot reveal all failure modes. A feature might work well on the 50 test cases your team created but fail catastrophically on the millionth real-world input. Without predefined kill criteria, teams often ship features that are "good enough" by subjective assessment, only to discover later that the failure rate is unacceptable.

Case Study: The "Good Enough" Trap

A fintech company built an AI that categorized transactions for expense reporting. The team tested with 200 transactions and achieved 92% accuracy, which they deemed "good enough" for launch. After shipping to 10,000 users, support tickets flooded in. The actual accuracy was only 74%, and worse, the errors followed patterns: the AI systematically miscategorized restaurant expenses as groceries. Users lost trust, and the company spent six months rebuilding the feature.

What went wrong: The team did not define minimum accuracy thresholds before launch. They tested on a small, unrepresentative sample. With predefined kill criteria (e.g., "90% accuracy across 5,000 test cases representing real usage distribution"), they would have caught the issue before shipping.

Minimum Accuracy Threshold

The minimum accuracy threshold defines what percentage of AI outputs must be correct for the feature to ship. This threshold should be based on user expectations and business impact. For a low-stakes feature like email drafting assistance, you might ship with 70% accuracy. For a high-stakes feature like medical diagnosis support, 95%+ accuracy would be required. The key is having this threshold defined before launch so the decision is objective rather than based on hope.

Skill: Setting Accuracy Thresholds

Consider these factors when setting accuracy thresholds:

  1. Stakes of error: What happens when the AI is wrong? Minor inconvenience or major harm?
  2. User expectations: What accuracy level would users expect for this task?
  3. Verification capability: Can users easily detect errors?
  4. Error recovery: How easily can users correct AI mistakes?

Example thresholds by use case:

  • Code completion: 80% acceptance rate (user keeps suggestion)
  • Customer support triage: 90% correct routing
  • Legal document review: 95% recall (catch all relevant documents)
  • Medical triage: 99% accuracy on critical decisions

Maximum Hallucination Rate

The maximum hallucination rate threshold defines the acceptable frequency of incorrect but confident outputs. Hallucinations are when an AI generates false information that sounds convincing (like making up a non-existent book citation). Hallucinations are particularly dangerous because they look correct to users who cannot verify the information. Different use cases have different hallucination tolerances: a travel recommendation that is wrong costs little, but legal advice that is wrong could be catastrophic. In general, any factual AI output should be verifiable, and the hallucination rate should be measured on verifiable facts.

Case Study: The Cost of Unchecked Hallucinations

A legal research AI company discovered that their system was hallucinating case citations. The AI would cite real-sounding case names with accurate-looking docket numbers, but the cases did not exist. Lawyers using the tool for research included these fake citations in court filings. When opposing counsel pointed out the nonexistent cases, the lawyers faced professional embarrassment and potential sanctions.

What they fixed: They implemented a hallucination rate limit: any factual claim must be verified against the retrieved documents. If the claim cannot be verified, the AI must either decline to make the claim or express uncertainty. The verification rate went from 65% to 99.5%.

Skill: Measuring Hallucination Rates

To measure hallucination rate, create a dataset of factual claims from AI outputs and verify each against ground truth. Track:

  • Verified correct: Claim matches ground truth
  • Verified incorrect: Claim contradicts ground truth (hallucination)
  • Unverifiable: Cannot confirm or deny (should not count as correct)

Hallucination rate formula: Incorrect / (Correct + Incorrect + Unverifiable)

Example targets by use case:

  • News summarization: <1% hallucination on verifiable facts
  • Product recommendations: <5% hallucination (subjective, less dangerous)
  • Code generation: <0.1% hallucination (executing wrong code is dangerous)

Response Time Limits

Response time limits define how slowly an AI can respond before the experience degrades below acceptable. Users have different expectations for different use cases: waiting 30 seconds for a complex analysis might be acceptable, but waiting 3 seconds for a simple autocomplete is not. Measure not just average response time but the distribution: a feature that usually responds in 1 second but occasionally takes 30 seconds is more problematic than one that consistently takes 3 seconds.

Case Study: The Long Tail Problem

A conversational AI product had an average response time of 1.2 seconds, which seemed acceptable. However, analyzing the distribution revealed that 8% of requests took more than 10 seconds. Users reported the product as "slow" and "unreliable" in surveys, even though most responses were fast. The long tail was destroying user experience.

What they fixed: They implemented p95 response time limits (95% of requests must complete within threshold). They also added timeout fallbacks: if a complex request will take too long, the AI returns a partial answer or offers to continue in background.

Skill: Setting Response Time Thresholds

Define both average and tail metrics:

  • p50 (median): Typical user experience
  • p95: What most users experience at their worst
  • p99: Outlier handling

Example thresholds by use case:

  • Search/completion: p95 <500ms
  • Chat interface: p95 <3 seconds
  • Complex analysis: p95 <30 seconds with progress indicator
  • Background processing: No limit, but status updates

Cost Per Interaction Targets

Cost per interaction targets define the maximum acceptable cost to serve each user interaction. AI products can have high marginal costs that affect the business model. Define cost targets early and monitor them throughout development. If you discover that serving each request costs $0.05 but your target is $0.01, you need to either optimize the system or reconsider the business model before launch.

Case Study: Unit Economics Failure

A B2B AI customer service startup priced their product at $29/user/month. They estimated serving cost at $0.02 per interaction. After launch, they discovered the average user generated 2,000 interactions monthly, for a serving cost of $40/user/month. They were losing $11 on every customer, every month. They had to raise prices 3x to break even.

What they fixed: They implemented tiered pricing based on interaction volume, added caching to reduce redundant API calls, and optimized prompt length to reduce token usage. The cost per interaction dropped to $0.015.

Skill: Cost Modeling for AI Products

Calculate your cost per interaction using this formula: 

Cost per interaction = 
  (Input tokens × input price) + 
  (Output tokens × output price) + 
  (Infrastructure cost / monthly interactions)

Example calculation:

  • Average input: 500 tokens at $0.001/1K = $0.0005
  • Average output: 200 tokens at $0.003/1K = $0.0006
  • Infrastructure (embedding, vector DB): $0.001 per request
  • Total cost per interaction: $0.0021

With this cost model, you can set pricing tiers that ensure profitability at each usage level.

Implementing Kill Criteria in Practice

Kill criteria should be documented in your product requirements and tracked throughout development. Create a kill criteria checklist that is reviewed before any launch decision. If any criterion is not met, the feature should not ship (or should ship only to a limited audience with explicit communication about limitations).
